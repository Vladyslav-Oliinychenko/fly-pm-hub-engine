# 3. SDLC and Delivery Frameworks (Run).

This section moves from the planning mechanics of the "Walk" phase to the dynamic, iterative execution of "Run." As a Project Manager in an IT outsourcing environment, you must master various Software Development Life Cycles (SDLC) and delivery frameworks to manage client expectations while protecting team health and project margins. This module provides a deep dive into the engineering, scaling, and flow principles that move a project from a Statement of Work (SOW) to a successful production release.

## 3.1. The Agile Mindset and Principles

Agile practices advocate for adaptive planning, incremental development, early delivery, continual improvement, and collaborative work with clients and end users. In outsourcing, this mindset is the most effective defense against the "obsolescence trap," where a product becomes irrelevant during a long development cycle because the client's market shifted while the vendor was stuck in a traditional, rigid process.

### 3.1.1. Agile Manifesto Values and Principles

The Agile Manifesto serves as the bedrock for modern delivery, establishing four core values that prioritize human interaction over rigid, contract-heavy structures.

* **Individuals and interactions over processes and tools.** Skilled engineers collaborating directly with client stakeholders deliver more value than a team strictly following a generic workflow.
* **Working software over comprehensive documentation.** The primary measure of progress is a functional product, not the volume of requirements documents or manuals.
* **Customer collaboration over contract negotiation.** Building trust and working together to solve problems is more important than focusing solely on legal agreements.
* **Responding to change over following a plan.** The ability to pivot based on feedback is a competitive advantage in a fast-moving market.

**Agile Principles.**
1. **Early and Continuous Delivery.** Satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software.
2. **Welcoming Change.** Harness change for the customer's competitive advantage, even late in development.
3. **Frequent Delivery.** Deliver working software frequently, from a couple of weeks to a couple of months.
4. **Business and Developers Working Together.** Stakeholders and developers must work together daily throughout the project.
5. **Motivated Individuals.** Build projects around motivated individuals, give them the environment they need, and trust them to get the job done.
6. **Face-to-Face Conversation.** The most efficient and effective method of conveying information is face-to-face conversation.
7. **Working Software.** Working software is the primary measure of progress.
8. **Sustainable Development.** Agile processes promote sustainable development, maintaining a constant pace indefinitely.
9. **Technical Excellence.** Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility.
10. **Simplicity.** The art of maximizing the amount of work not done is essential.
11. **Self-Organizing Teams.** The best architectures, requirements, and designs emerge from self-organizing teams.
12. **Regular Reflection.** At regular intervals, the team reflects on how to become more effective, then tunes and adjusts its behavior accordingly.

## 3.2. Software Development Life Cycles (SDLC)

An SDLC is a framework defining tasks performed at each step in the software development process. Choosing the right SDLC is a critical decision for a Project Manager.

### 3.2.1. Waterfall (Linear/Sequential)

* **Concept.** A rigid, sequential approach where each phase (Requirements → Design → Implementation → Verification → Maintenance) must be completed before the next begins.
* **Best for.** Projects with fixed requirements, strict regulatory environments, or short, simple deliverables where the scope is 100% known.
* **Risk.** High risk of failure if requirements change, as testing only happens at the very end.

### 3.2.2. V-Model (Validation & Verification)

* **Concept.** An extension of the waterfall model where each development stage has a corresponding testing stage (e.g., Unit Testing for Coding, System Testing for System Design).
* **Best for.** Systems where safety and reliability are paramount (e.g., medical devices, aviation).
* **Risk.** Still rigid and difficult to adapt to changes once development is underway.

### 3.2.3. Iterative and Incremental

* **Concept.** The project is broken into small parts (increments). Each increment goes through the entire SDLC. The system is built and improved through repeated cycles (iterations).
* **Best for.** Large projects where some requirements are known, but the full vision is still evolving.

### 3.2.4. Spiral Model

* **Concept.** A risk-driven model that combines iterative development with systematic aspects of the waterfall model. It places heavy emphasis on risk assessment.
* **Best for.** High-risk, large-scale, complex industrial projects.

## 3.3. Core Agile Frameworks

### 3.3.1. Scrum

Scrum is the most popular Agile framework, used by the majority of IT delivery teams globally.

* **Roles.** The Scrum Team consists of a Product Owner, a Scrum Master, and Developers.
* **Events.**
    * **Sprint.** A fixed-length event of one month or less.
    * **Sprint Planning.** Sets the work to be performed.
    * **Daily Scrum.** A 15-minute event for the developers to inspect progress toward the Sprint Goal.
    * **Sprint Review.** Inspects the outcome of the Sprint and determines future adaptations.
    * **Sprint Retrospective.** Plans ways to increase quality and effectiveness.
* **Artifacts.** Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, and Increment.

### 3.3.2. Kanban

* **Concept.** A method for managing the flow of work with an emphasis on continuous delivery without overburdening the team.
* **Core Practices.** Visualize the workflow, limit work in progress (WIP), manage flow, make process policies explicit, and improve collaboratively.
* **Metric.** Lead Time (time from request to delivery) and Cycle Time (time from work start to delivery).

### 3.3.3. Scrumban

* **Concept.** A hybrid framework that combines the structure of Scrum (roles, events) with the visualization and flow-based flexibility of Kanban.
* **Best for.** Maintenance projects or teams that find the rigidity of Sprints difficult but still want Scrum governance.

### 3.3.4. Extreme Programming (XP)

* **Concept.** An Agile framework aimed at improving software quality and responsiveness to changing customer requirements through high-quality engineering practices.
* **Practices.** Pair Programming, Test-Driven Development (TDD), Continuous Integration, and Small Releases.

## 3.4. Engineering Excellence and Technical Debt

In outsourcing, delivering "fast" at the expense of "good" creates long-term liability for the client and the vendor.

### 3.4.1. Technical Debt

* **Definition.** The implied cost of additional rework caused by choosing an easy (fast) solution now instead of using a better approach that would take longer.
* **The PM's Role.** Negotiate with the Product Owner to allocate time (usually 10-20% of a sprint) to "repay" debt by refactoring code and fixing non-critical bugs.

### 3.4.2. CI/CD (Continuous Integration and Delivery)

* **Continuous Integration (CI).** Developers frequently merge their code changes into a central repository, where automated builds and tests are run.
* **Continuous Delivery (CD).** Code changes are automatically prepared for a release to production.

### 3.4.3. TDD and BDD

* **Test-Driven Development (TDD).** Writing a failing test before writing the code to make it pass.
* **Behavior-Driven Development (BDD).** Writing tests in a natural language that non-programmers can understand (e.g., Given/When/Then), focusing on the business behavior.

## 3.5. Quality Assurance and Testing Strategies

### 3.5.1. The Testing Pyramid

A strategy for building a robust automated test suite.
* **Unit Tests.** Large base of fast, automated tests covering small pieces of code.
* **Integration Tests.** Middle layer testing how components work together.
* **End-to-End (E2E) / UI Tests.** Small top layer testing the full user journey (slowest and most expensive).

### 3.5.2. Shift-Left Testing

* **Concept.** Moving testing as early as possible in the SDLC.
* **Benefit.** Finding a bug during the design phase costs significantly less than finding it in production.

## 3.6. Scaling Frameworks

When a project grows from one team to ten, standard Scrum breaks down. Scaling frameworks provide the structure to align multiple teams toward a single goal.

### 3.6.1. SAFe (Scaled Agile Framework)

* **Concept.** A highly structured, corporate-friendly framework that aligns teams through "Agile Release Trains" (ARTs).
* **Key Event.** PI Planning (Program Increment Planning), a two-day event where all teams plan their work for the next 8-12 weeks.
* **Best for.** Large enterprises needing high predictability and top-down alignment.

### 3.6.2. LeSS (Large-Scale Scrum)

* **Concept.** "Scrum applied to many teams." It keeps the structure minimal, with one Product Owner and one Backlog for all teams.
* **Best for.** Organizations that want to scale without adding heavy layers of management.

### 3.6.3. Scrum at Scale (ScS)

* **Concept.** Created by Jeff Sutherland, it focuses on a "Scale-Free" architecture using a Scrum of Scrums (SoS) to coordinate between teams.

### 3.6.4. Disciplined Agile (DA)

* **Concept.** A process-decision toolkit that provides guidance to help organizations choose their "Way of Working" (WoW).

### 3.6.5. Nexus

* **Concept.** A framework for multiple teams (3 to 9) working on a single product.
* **Nexus Integration Team.** A dedicated role to ensure the integrated increment is delivered.

**Real-Life Example: Integration First.** Pentacomp used the Nexus framework to develop an e-health prescription platform for the Ministry of Health in Poland. With nearly 100 developers across 9 teams, they faced massive integration challenges. By forming a Nexus Integration Team and synchronizing through Nexus events, they delivered a shippable product that became critical during the pandemic, allowing patients to get prescriptions without physical visits.

### 3.6.6. The Spotify Model and UnFix

* **Spotify.** A cultural model focusing on autonomy. It uses terms like Squads (dev teams), Tribes (40-150 people), Chapters (technology specialists within a tribe), and Guilds (communities of interest). A Tribe Trio consisting of a Tribe Lead, Product Lead, and Design Lead aligns the work.
* **UnFix.** A pattern library for organizational design that allows companies to build flexible, tailored structures rather than adopting a fixed framework.

**Real-Life Example: Squad Autonomy.** A digital music company organized its vendor engineers into squads. Each squad was given a mission, such as make search faster, and the autonomy to choose their own tools. To prevent the teams from becoming silos, they created chapters for all developers to share coding tips. This allowed the company to innovate quickly while maintaining high technical standards across the organization.
